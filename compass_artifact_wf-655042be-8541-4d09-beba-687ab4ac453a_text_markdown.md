# Common English Mistakes by Spanish Speakers: A Comprehensive Reference for Dialogo

## TL;DR
- The single most frequent error category among Spanish-speaking English learners is **verb-related** (misuse of verbs accounted for 20% of Spanish-interference errors in Cabrera Solano et al.'s 2014 study of 351 Ecuadorian high schoolers), followed by **pronoun omission (16%), preposition misuse (11%), article overuse (9%) and word-order errors (9%)** — so a flashcard app should weight content roughly in that order.
- Most errors stem from **direct translation from Spanish (L1 interference)**: 76% of students in Cabrera Solano et al. (2014) reported "thinking in Spanish first and then translating their ideas into English," which explains errors like "I have 25 years," "depend of," "married with," "people is," and adjective-after-noun word order.
- For pronunciation and spelling, the highest-impact targets are the **/v/–/b/ merger, /θ/ and /ð/ (th) substitutions, the schwa /ə/, /ɪ/–/iː/ (ship/sheep), e-epenthesis before /s/-clusters (estudent), and consonant-cluster reduction** — these are documented in Joseph Hudson's "10 English Pronunciation Errors by Spanish Speakers" (Pronunciation Studio, last updated March 2018) and in Hevia-Tuero, Russak & Suárez-Coalla's (*Reading and Writing* 36(7), 2023) finding that orthographic errors made up 60.30% of all spelling mistakes in 136 Spanish primary schoolers.

## Key Findings

This reference catalogues **109 individual errors** across 12 categories, ranked within each category from most common/impactful to least common, drawn from peer-reviewed error-analysis studies (Cabrera Solano et al., 2014; Hevia-Tuero et al., 2023; Hasbún, 2009), Cambridge's standard teacher reference *Learner English* (Swan & Smith, 2001), and major ESL teaching resources. Each entry follows a fixed schema:

> **❌ Error:** what learners say  →  **✅ Correct:** target form  |  **🇪🇸 Source:** Spanish that causes the interference  |  **💡 Why:** brief explanation.

## Details

### 1. Verb-Tense Errors (highest-frequency category — misuse of verbs = 20% of Spanish-interference errors per Cabrera Solano et al., 2014)

1. **❌** "I have lived here since five years" / "I live here for five years" → **✅** "I have lived here for five years." | **🇪🇸** *Vivo aquí desde hace cinco años* | **💡** Spanish uses present tense + *desde hace* for ongoing situations; English requires the present perfect.
2. **❌** "I have seen him yesterday." → **✅** "I saw him yesterday." | **🇪🇸** *Lo he visto ayer* (acceptable in peninsular Spanish) | **💡** Spaniards extend the present perfect to finished time-frames; English never combines present perfect with a finished time marker.
3. **❌** "When I will arrive, I'll call you." → **✅** "When I arrive, I'll call you." | **🇪🇸** *Cuando llegaré, te llamaré* | **💡** English uses present simple in future time clauses introduced by *when, after, before, as soon as, until, if*.
4. **❌** "I am knowing him for years." / "I am wanting a coffee." → **✅** "I have known him for years." / "I want a coffee." | **🇪🇸** *Lo estoy conociendo / Estoy queriendo un café* | **💡** Stative verbs (know, want, believe, understand, love, need, see, hear) don't normally take the progressive in English.
5. **❌** "He no come yesterday." / "Where you went?" → **✅** "He didn't come yesterday." / "Where did you go?" | **🇪🇸** *Él no vino ayer / ¿Adónde fuiste?* | **💡** Spanish has no *do*-support; the auxiliary *do/did* is the most-omitted function word in negation and questions.
6. **❌** "She no like coffee." / "He live in Madrid." → **✅** "She doesn't like coffee." / "He lives in Madrid." | **🇪🇸** *Ella no toma café / Él vive en Madrid* | **💡** Third-person -*s* is the only mandatory present-tense ending in English, and Spanish learners routinely drop it because Spanish marks person on every verb.
7. **❌** "I can to speak English." / "You should to study." → **✅** "I can speak English." / "You should study." | **🇪🇸** *Puedo hablar inglés / Deberías estudiar* | **💡** Spanish *poder/deber* take an infinitive; English modals take a bare infinitive (no *to*).
8. **❌** "If I would have money, I would buy it." → **✅** "If I had money, I would buy it." | **🇪🇸** *Si tuviera dinero, lo compraría* | **💡** The Spanish past subjunctive maps onto English simple past in second-conditional *if*-clauses, not *would*.
9. **❌** "I am born in Mexico." → **✅** "I was born in Mexico." | **🇪🇸** *Nací en México* / *Soy nacido en México* | **💡** *Be born* is passive past in English, but Spanish often uses present *ser* or a simple preterite.
10. **❌** "He is dead since 1990." → **✅** "He has been dead since 1990." / "He died in 1990." | **🇪🇸** *Está muerto desde 1990* | **💡** Spanish uses adjectival *estar* + participle for resultant states; English requires the present perfect.
11. **❌** "I am here since Monday." → **✅** "I have been here since Monday." | **🇪🇸** *Estoy aquí desde el lunes* | **💡** Same pattern as #1; Spanish present-with-*desde* maps onto English present perfect continuous.
12. **❌** "I was working when she has called." → **✅** "I was working when she called." | **🇪🇸** *Estaba trabajando cuando ella ha llamado* | **💡** Sequence-of-tense interference from Spain's "hot-news" perfect.

### 2. Pronoun & Subject Errors (omission of personal and object pronouns = 16% of interference errors per Cabrera Solano et al., 2014)

13. **❌** "Is raining." / "Bought food yesterday." → **✅** "It is raining." / "I/She bought food yesterday." | **🇪🇸** *Está lloviendo / Compré comida ayer* | **💡** Spanish is a pro-drop language (subject encoded in verb endings); English requires an explicit subject — including dummy *it* and *there*.
14. **❌** "Is a problem." → **✅** "There is a problem." | **🇪🇸** *Hay un problema* | **💡** Existential *hay* has no overt subject; English needs *there*.
15. **❌** "Listen me!" / "Tell to him." → **✅** "Listen to me!" / "Tell him." | **🇪🇸** *¡Escúchame! / Dile* | **💡** Spanish object clitics (*me, le*) attach to the verb without prepositions; English verbs differ verb by verb in whether they take *to*.
16. **❌** "Janet left **his** jacket behind." (referring to a woman) → **✅** "Janet left **her** jacket behind." | **🇪🇸** *Janet dejó su chaqueta* | **💡** Spanish *su* covers his/her/your/their; English possessive agrees with the *possessor's* gender, not the object's.
17. **❌** "She gave to me the book." → **✅** "She gave me the book." / "She gave the book to me." | **🇪🇸** *Me dio el libro / Le dio el libro a mí* | **💡** Spanish requires preposition *a* before indirect objects; English allows the bare double-object construction.
18. **❌** "The girl that I told you about her..." → **✅** "The girl I told you about..." | **🇪🇸** *La chica de la que te hablé de ella* | **💡** Spanish often resumes the relative pronoun with a pronoun; English doesn't.
19. **❌** "Me and my brother went..." (in formal writing) → **✅** "My brother and I went..." | **🇪🇸** *Yo y mi hermano fuimos* | **💡** Politeness order and case in compound subjects.

### 3. Preposition Errors (misuse of prepositions = 11% of interference errors per Cabrera Solano et al., 2014; one of the longest-lived errors per Heather Hughes' ESL practice notes and Pronunciation Studio's diagnostic)

20. **❌** "It depend**s of** the weather." → **✅** "It depends **on** the weather." | **🇪🇸** *Depende del tiempo* | **💡** Spanish *depender de* maps the wrong preposition; *on* signals reliance in English.
21. **❌** "I'm married **with** her." → **✅** "I'm married **to** her." | **🇪🇸** *Casado con ella* | **💡** Direct translation of *con*; English uses *to*.
22. **❌** "I'm looking ___ my keys." → **✅** "I'm looking **for** my keys." | **🇪🇸** *Busco mis llaves* | **💡** Spanish *buscar* encodes "look for" in the verb itself, so the preposition is dropped.
23. **❌** "I'm listening ___ music." → **✅** "I'm listening **to** music." | **🇪🇸** *Escucho música* | **💡** Same omission pattern; *escuchar* needs no preposition in Spanish.
24. **❌** "I arrived **to** Madrid." → **✅** "I arrived **in** Madrid." (cities/countries) / "I arrived **at** the station." | **🇪🇸** *Llegué a Madrid* | **💡** Spanish *a* covers both English *in* (large places) and *at* (specific points).
25. **❌** "Put the food **in** the plate." → **✅** "Put the food **on** the plate." | **🇪🇸** *Pon la comida en el plato* | **💡** Spanish *en* maps to English *in, on,* and *at*; surface (plate) requires *on*.
26. **❌** "**On** the morning" → **✅** "**In** the morning." | **🇪🇸** *En la mañana* | **💡** Same Spanish *en*; English uses *in* with parts of the day except *at night*.
27. **❌** "I think **on/in** you every day." → **✅** "I think **about/of** you every day." | **🇪🇸** *Pienso en ti* | **💡** Mismatched dependent preposition.
28. **❌** "I'm interested **on** music." → **✅** "I'm interested **in** music." | **🇪🇸** *Estoy interesado en la música* | **💡** Dependent-preposition mismatch despite the seemingly close Spanish *en*.
29. **❌** "I agree **to** you." → **✅** "I agree **with** you." | **🇪🇸** *Estoy de acuerdo contigo* | **💡** Spanish *contigo* prompts wrong preposition transfer.
30. **❌** "Consist **in**" → **✅** "Consist **of**." | **🇪🇸** *Consistir en* | **💡** Common in formal/academic writing.
31. **❌** "I helped **to** my parents." → **✅** "I helped my parents." | **🇪🇸** *Ayudé a mis padres* | **💡** Spanish "personal *a*" inserts a preposition that English doesn't need.
32. **❌** "She entered **to/in** the room." → **✅** "She entered the room." | **🇪🇸** *Entró a/en la habitación* | **💡** *Enter* is transitive in English.
33. **❌** "It's different **of/to** mine." → **✅** "It's different **from** mine." (or *than* in US English) | **🇪🇸** *Diferente de* | **💡** Wrong preposition mapping.

### 4. Article and Determiner Errors (Cabrera Solano et al., 2014 report **overuse of articles = 9%** and **misuse of articles = 5%** as two separate top-frequency categories; Hasbún 2009 found that **overuse of the definite article accounted for 40–58.82% of all article errors** across eight proficiency groups of 159 University of Costa Rica EFL students, and that article errors as a whole represented 12.01%–16.65% of all grammar errors)

34. **❌** "**The** life is hard." / "**The** people are kind." → **✅** "Life is hard." / "People are kind." | **🇪🇸** *La vida es dura / La gente es amable* | **💡** Spanish uses definite articles with generic/abstract nouns; English uses zero article for general reference. This is the single biggest article error and dominates Hasbún's data.
35. **❌** "I like **the** dogs." (meaning dogs in general) → **✅** "I like dogs." | **🇪🇸** *Me gustan los perros* | **💡** Plural generic = zero article in English.
36. **❌** "I'm ___ teacher." → **✅** "I'm **a** teacher." | **🇪🇸** *Soy profesor* | **💡** Spanish omits the indefinite article before professions; English requires it.
37. **❌** "I have ___ headache." → **✅** "I have **a** headache." | **🇪🇸** *Tengo dolor de cabeza* | **💡** Indefinite article required with countable singular ailments.
38. **❌** "**The** Dr. Smith called." → **✅** "Dr. Smith called." | **🇪🇸** *El Dr. Smith llamó* | **💡** Spanish uses *el/la* before titles; English drops the article.
39. **❌** "**The** Mr. President." → **✅** "Mr. President." / "The President." | **🇪🇸** *El señor Presidente* | **💡** Title + name has no article in English.
40. **❌** "I play **the** football." → **✅** "I play football." | **🇪🇸** *Juego al fútbol* | **💡** Spanish *al* (a + el) prompts a definite article that English drops with most sports.
41. **❌** "I go to **the** school every day." (as a student) → **✅** "I go to school every day." | **🇪🇸** *Voy a la escuela* | **💡** Functional institutions (school, church, work, bed, prison) use zero article in their generic role.
42. **❌** "**On the** Monday" → **✅** "On Monday." | **🇪🇸** *El lunes* | **💡** Spanish days of the week always take *el*; English uses zero article with *on*.
43. **❌** "I speak **the** English." → **✅** "I speak English." | **🇪🇸** *Hablo el inglés* | **💡** Language names take zero article unless modified.
44. **❌** "For **the** breakfast I had toast." → **✅** "For breakfast I had toast." | **🇪🇸** *Para el desayuno* | **💡** Meals take zero article.

### 5. False Cognates / False Friends (~4% of errors in Cabrera Solano et al., 2014 but among the most communication-damaging)

45. **❌** "I'm **embarrassed**" used to mean pregnant. → **✅** "I'm pregnant" / "I'm embarrassed (= ashamed)." | **🇪🇸** *embarazada = pregnant* | **💡** The classic false friend; *embarrassed* = *avergonzado*.
46. **❌** "I **assist** to class every day." → **✅** "I **attend** class every day." | **🇪🇸** *Asistir a = to attend* | **💡** English *assist* = *ayudar*.
47. **❌** "I will **realize** my dream." (meaning achieve) → **✅** "I will **achieve/fulfill** my dream." | **🇪🇸** *Realizar = to carry out/achieve* | **💡** English *realize* = *darse cuenta de*.
48. **❌** "**Actually**, I live in Madrid." (meaning currently) → **✅** "**Currently**, I live in Madrid." | **🇪🇸** *Actualmente = currently* | **💡** English *actually* = *de hecho/en realidad*.
49. **❌** "He is very **sensible**." (meaning sensitive) → **✅** "He is very **sensitive**." | **🇪🇸** *Sensible = sensitive* | **💡** English *sensible* = *sensato/razonable*.
50. **❌** "I'm **constipated**." (meaning I have a cold) → **✅** "I have **a cold**." | **🇪🇸** *Constipado = head cold* | **💡** English *constipated* = *estreñido*.
51. **❌** "Let's go to the **library**." (meaning bookstore) → **✅** "Let's go to the **bookstore**." | **🇪🇸** *Librería = bookstore* | **💡** English *library* = *biblioteca*.
52. **❌** "I have an **exit** at 8pm." (meaning outing) → **✅** "I have an outing at 8pm." | **🇪🇸** *Una salida* | **💡** Mistranslating *salida*.
53. **❌** "I **support** the heat." (meaning tolerate) → **✅** "I can **stand/tolerate** the heat." | **🇪🇸** *Soportar = to tolerate* | **💡** English *support* = *apoyar*.
54. **❌** "My **carpet** is full of papers." (meaning folder) → **✅** "My **folder** is full of papers." | **🇪🇸** *Carpeta = folder* | **💡** English *carpet* = *alfombra*.
55. **❌** "I'm going to **discuss** with my boss." (meaning argue) → **✅** "I'm going to **argue with** my boss." | **🇪🇸** *Discutir* often = *argue* | **💡** English *discuss* is neutral; Spanish *discutir* often combative.
56. **❌** "**Eventually**" used to mean possibly. → **✅** "*Eventually* = in the end; *possibly* = *quizás*." | **🇪🇸** *Eventualmente = possibly* | **💡** Total semantic mismatch.
57. **❌** "**Molest**" used for *bother*. → **✅** "**Bother**." | **🇪🇸** *Molestar = to bother* | **💡** English *molest* has narrowed to sexual abuse; this is the highest-stakes false friend.
58. **❌** "I'm going to **introduce** the key in the lock." → **✅** "I'm going to **insert** the key." | **🇪🇸** *Introducir = to insert* | **💡** English *introduce* = present a person/topic.
59. **❌** "**Exit**" used as a verb instead of *leave*. → **✅** "Leave." | **🇪🇸** *Salir / Éxito (success)* | **💡** *Exit* is rarely a verb in conversation, and the spelling-similar *éxito* is a separate false friend.

### 6. Idiom/Fixed-Phrase Errors (calques)

60. **❌** "I **have** 25 years." → **✅** "I **am** 25 years old." | **🇪🇸** *Tengo 25 años* | **💡** Spanish uses *tener* for age, hunger, thirst, cold, heat, fear, sleep; English uses *be*.
61. **❌** "I have hunger / I have cold / I have fear / I have sleep." → **✅** "I'm hungry / cold / afraid / sleepy." | **🇪🇸** *Tengo hambre / frío / miedo / sueño* | **💡** Same *tener*-based idiom set.
62. **❌** "**Have** reason." → **✅** "**Be** right." | **🇪🇸** *Tener razón* | **💡** Same idiom pattern.
63. **❌** "How is called this?" / "How do you call this?" → **✅** "What is this called?" / "What do you call this?" | **🇪🇸** *¿Cómo se llama esto?* | **💡** Spanish *cómo* maps to English *what* when asking for names/descriptions.
64. **❌** "How **does it look like**?" → **✅** "**What** does it look like?" | **🇪🇸** *¿Cómo es?* | **💡** Same *cómo/what* mismatch.
65. **❌** "Can you **borrow** me a pen?" → **✅** "Can you **lend** me a pen?" | **🇪🇸** *prestar* covers both directions | **💡** *Borrow* = receive; *lend* = give.
66. **❌** "**Make** a question." → **✅** "**Ask** a question." | **🇪🇸** *Hacer una pregunta* | **💡** Spanish *hacer* covers *make/do/ask*.
67. **❌** "**Do** a party / a mistake / a photo / a decision." → **✅** "**Throw/have** a party / **make** a mistake / **take** a photo / **make** a decision." | **🇪🇸** *Hacer una fiesta / un error / una foto* | **💡** Spanish single verb *hacer* splits into multiple English collocations.
68. **❌** "**Do** a cake." → **✅** "**Make** a cake." (and: *do* homework, *do* sport, *make* the bed) | **🇪🇸** *hacer* covers all | **💡** Rule of thumb: *make* = create/produce; *do* = perform/complete an activity.
69. **❌** "I'm **agree** with you." → **✅** "I **agree** with you." | **🇪🇸** *Estoy de acuerdo* | **💡** Spanish uses *estar + de acuerdo* (an adjectival phrase); English *agree* is a verb, not an adjective.
70. **❌** "It's **raining cats and dogs**" attempted literally from Spanish weather idioms. → **✅** Learn English idioms separately. | **🇪🇸** *Llueve a cántaros* | **💡** Idioms rarely translate literally.

### 7. Word-Order Errors (incorrect/unnatural word order = 9% of interference errors per Cabrera Solano et al., 2014)

71. **❌** "She is a girl **very nice**." / "I bought a car **red**." → **✅** "She is a **very nice** girl." / "I bought a **red car**." | **🇪🇸** *Una chica muy simpática / un coche rojo* | **💡** Spanish places most descriptive adjectives after the noun; English places them before.
72. **❌** "**Always I** drink coffee." → **✅** "I **always** drink coffee." | **🇪🇸** *Siempre tomo café* | **💡** Adverbs of frequency in English go between subject and main verb (or after *be*).
73. **❌** "Yesterday went I to the cinema." → **✅** "Yesterday I went to the cinema." | **🇪🇸** *Ayer fui al cine* | **💡** Spanish allows subject-verb inversion freely; English keeps SVO.
74. **❌** "Tell me **what time is it**." → **✅** "Tell me **what time it is**." | **🇪🇸** *Dime qué hora es* | **💡** Embedded questions in English revert to statement order.

### 8. Pronunciation Errors That Affect Communication

(Source: Joseph Hudson, "10 English Pronunciation Errors by Spanish Speakers," Pronunciation Studio (London), first published December 2013, last updated March 2018; Hevia-Tuero et al., 2023, in *Reading and Writing* 36(7), where the single most-frequent specific spelling error code was "grapheme doubling" at 15.05% of all errors — e.g., *rabit* for *rabbit*.)

75. **/v/ pronounced as /b/** — *very* → "berry," *seven* → "seben." Spanish *b/v* are allophones. **Impact:** very high; merges minimal pairs.
76. **/θ/ and /ð/ (th)** — *thirsty* → "tirsty/firsty," *brother* → "broder." These sounds don't exist in Latin-American Spanish.
77. **e-epenthesis before /s/-clusters** — *Spain* → "Espain," *student* → "estudent," *strike* → "estrike." Spanish words never start with /s/+consonant.
78. **/ɪ/ vs /iː/ (ship/sheep)** — Spanish has only one /i/; this is the #1 vowel issue in Pronunciation Studio's clinical ranking. Leads to mergers of *ship/sheep, bit/beat, live/leave*.
79. **/æ/ vs /ʌ/ vs /ɑː/** — *cat/cut/cart* merged. Spanish has only one open /a/.
80. **Schwa /ə/** — Spanish speakers pronounce every vowel fully ("com-PU-ter" instead of /kəmˈpjuːtə/). Schwa is the most common English sound and is foreign to Spanish.
81. **/h/ omitted or pronounced as Spanish /x/** — *hello* silent or *house* → "jouse." Spanish *h* is silent; *j* is /x/.
82. **Final consonant clusters reduced** — *task* → "tas," *next* → "nes," *worked* → "work." Spanish syllables typically end in vowels.
83. **/z/ pronounced as /s/** — *zoo* → "soo," *please* → "pleese." Spanish has no /z/.
84. **/dʒ/ vs /j/** — *yes/Jess*, *yet/jet* confusion.
85. **Word stress shifted to Spanish patterns** — *photograph* stressed on the last syllable, *hotel* on the first.
86. **Final /-d/ devoiced or dropped** — *bad* → "bat," *cold* → "col."

### 9. Subject-Verb Agreement Errors

87. **❌** "People **is** kind." → **✅** "People **are** kind." | **🇪🇸** *La gente es amable* | **💡** Spanish *gente* is singular; English *people* is irregular plural.
88. **❌** "Everybody **have** their book." → **✅** "Everybody **has** their book." | **🇪🇸** *Todos tienen su libro* | **💡** *Everybody, everyone, somebody, anybody, no one* are grammatically singular in English.
89. **❌** "My family **are** big." (in US English) → **✅** "My family **is** big." | **🇪🇸** *Mi familia es grande* | **💡** Collective nouns are usually singular in American English (British English allows plural).
90. **❌** "She **don't** know." → **✅** "She **doesn't** know." | **🇪🇸** *Ella no sabe* | **💡** Third-person -*s* on auxiliary *do*.
91. **❌** "There **is** many books." → **✅** "There **are** many books." | **🇪🇸** *Hay muchos libros* | **💡** Spanish *hay* is invariant; English *there is/are* must agree with the following noun.

### 10. Adjective-Specific Errors

92. **❌** "The film was very **bored**." → **✅** "The film was very **boring**." | **🇪🇸** *La película fue aburrida* | **💡** -*ing* adjectives describe the cause; -*ed* adjectives describe the experiencer. Same applies to *interested/interesting, excited/exciting, tired/tiring, surprised/surprising, confused/confusing, frustrated/frustrating*.
93. **❌** "She is **embarrassing**." (meaning she feels embarrassed) → **✅** "She is **embarrassed**." | **🇪🇸** *Ella está avergonzada* | **💡** Same -*ed*/-*ing* pattern but with high social cost.
94. **❌** "The class is **funny**." (meaning enjoyable) → **✅** "The class is **fun**." | **🇪🇸** *La clase es divertida* | **💡** *Fun* = enjoyable; *funny* = laugh-inducing or strange.
95. **❌** "The **reds** cars." → **✅** "The **red** cars." | **🇪🇸** *Los coches rojos* | **💡** Spanish adjectives agree in number; English adjectives are invariant.
96. **❌** "He is more **tall** than me." → **✅** "He is **taller** than me." | **🇪🇸** *Más alto que yo* | **💡** Short adjectives take -*er*; Spanish only has the *más* construction.
97. **❌** "It is **more better**." → **✅** "It is **better**." | **🇪🇸** *Es mucho mejor* | **💡** Double comparative.

### 11. Double Negatives

98. **❌** "I **don't** want **nothing**." / "I **don't** know **nobody**." → **✅** "I don't want anything." / "I don't know anybody." | **🇪🇸** *No quiero nada / No conozco a nadie* | **💡** Spanish requires double negation; English allows only one negative element per clause.
99. **❌** "I **never** **don't** go there." → **✅** "I never go there." | **🇪🇸** *Nunca no voy allí* (rare) | **💡** Same double-negative interference.

### 12. Spelling Errors from Spanish Interference

(Hevia-Tuero, Russak & Suárez-Coalla, 2023, in *Reading and Writing* 36:1797–1820 (DOI 10.1007/s11145-022-10356-5), analyzed 965 errors in writing by 136 Spanish primary schoolers, grades 4–6, and reported: **orthographic errors = 60.30% (SD = 27.38), phonological errors = 25.58% (SD = 23.20)**, with **grapheme doubling errors alone making up 15.05% (SD = 23.31)** of all spelling errors — the single most common error code — long-vowel digraph misrepresentation **9.56% (SD = 17.08)**, epenthesis **6.13% (SD = 12.91)**, and cluster reduction **5.36% (SD = 12.73)**.)

100. **❌** *rabit, hapy, comunication, profesion* → **✅** *rabbit, happy, communication, profession*. Spanish almost never doubles consonants except *rr, ll, cc, nn*; learners under-double in English. **(Most frequent specific spelling error: 15.05% per Hevia-Tuero et al., 2023.)**
101. **❌** *creem, beleeve, recieve* → **✅** *cream, believe, receive*. Long-vowel digraphs (*ea, ie, ei*) misrepresented because Spanish vowels have a 1:1 letter-sound correspondence. (9.56% of errors.)
102. **❌** *fone, foto, fisical* → **✅** *phone, photo, physical*. Spanish replaces all *ph* with *f*.
103. **❌** *qualifie, qualyti* → **✅** *qualify, quality*. *Qu* + vowel patterns differ.
104. **❌** *aprove, oportunity, posible* → **✅** *approve, opportunity, possible*. Single-consonant interference.
105. **❌** Missing silent letters: *now* for *know*, *our* for *hour*. Spanish has no silent letters except *h*.
106. **❌** *broter, brodher, tink* → **✅** *brother, think*. *Th* spelling absent from Spanish.
107. **❌** *Espain, estudent, esport* → **✅** *Spain, student, sport*. Written-form spillover of e-epenthesis.
108. **❌** *frien, alway* → **✅** *friend, always*. Final-cluster reduction reflected in writing (cluster reduction = 5.36% of all errors per Hevia-Tuero et al., 2023).
109. **❌** *like's* for *likes* — apostrophe inserted before plural/third-person *-s* (apostrophe-addition errors = 2.98% per Hevia-Tuero et al., 2023).

## Recommendations (for the Dialogo flashcard app)

**Stage 1 — Build the core deck (top 30 errors).** Front-load the deck around the highest-frequency categories: verb tenses (#1–12), pronoun/subject omissions (#13–19), prepositions (#20–33), and articles (#34–44). These four categories account for roughly 56% of all Spanish-interference errors documented in Cabrera Solano et al. (2014). Each card should show the ❌/✅/🇪🇸/💡 schema so the learner *sees* the L1 trigger.

**Stage 2 — Add false-friend and idiom decks.** False cognates (#45–59) and *tener*/*hacer* calques (#60–70) are lower in raw frequency but higher in social cost — *embarazada*, *molest*, *constipated*, "I have 25 years" — so they're the highest-value content for intermediate learners aiming at confidence and culturally safe communication.

**Stage 3 — Pronunciation drilling.** Build a separate audio module covering the eight highest-impact sounds (#75–82). Use **minimal-pair quizzes** (ship/sheep, very/berry, thin/tin) rather than isolated phonemes; that's the format Joseph Hudson recommends in the Pronunciation Studio clinical diagnostic.

**Stage 4 — Spelling and adjective-pair quizzes.** Multiple-choice items work best for -*ed*/-*ing* adjectives (#92–93), consonant-doubling spelling (#100–101), and *make/do* collocations (#67–68). The Hevia-Tuero data argue specifically for hammering grapheme doubling, since it alone produces 15% of all spelling errors.

**Benchmarks that should change the roadmap:**
- If user analytics show **>30% error rate on present-perfect items after 4 weeks**, add a dedicated *since/for/already/yet* module — that's the deepest-rooted tense error and is specifically flagged by Leonardo English and Heather Hughes' ESL blogs as the highest-impact intermediate-level verb-tense issue.
- If **preposition accuracy plateaus around 75–80%**, switch from rule teaching to **collocation chunks** ("depend on," "interested in," "married to"); that plateau pattern is described in the Cucmanh 2025 teaching summary and lines up with the documented 11% raw frequency.
- If **article-omission errors begin to exceed article-overuse errors** in user data, you have a more advanced cohort: Hasbún (2009) found overuse dominates at lower proficiency, dropping from 58.82% to 40.00% of all article errors between beginner and advanced groups — a clean proficiency signal you can use to trigger harder material.

## Caveats

- **Frequency ranking is approximate.** The 20%/16%/11%/9%/9%/5% figures come specifically from Cabrera Solano, P. A., Gonzalez Torres, P. F., Ochoa Cueva, C. A., Quinonez Beltran, A. L., Castillo Cuesta, L. M., Solano Jaramillo, L. M., Espinosa Jaramillo, F. O., & Arias Cordova, M. O. (2014), "Spanish Interference in EFL Writing Skills: A Case of Ecuadorian Senior High Schools," *English Language Teaching* 7(7), 40–48 (Canadian Center of Science and Education; DOI 10.5539/elt.v7n7p40), based on 351 Ecuadorian senior-high-school *writers* in Loja. Rankings in *speech*, in adult learners, or in different dialects (Caribbean, peninsular, US Heritage) will shift somewhat. The present-perfect/preterite error, for example, is much more salient for peninsular Spanish speakers than for Latin Americans, who already prefer the simple past.
- **Some teaching-blog statistics should be treated as illustrative, not empirical.** Claims such as "Spanish speakers improved 23% more with preposition-focused training" appear in commercial blogs (e.g., Cucmanh, 2025) without primary citations.
- **Pronunciation interference varies by Spanish dialect**: /θ/ exists in Castilian (Spain) Spanish but not in most Latin-American varieties; /j/ vs /dʒ/ varies between Río de la Plata and Mexican Spanish; final /s/ aspiration is common in Caribbean Spanish and affects English /s/ production.
- **Heritage speakers (US-born Spanish/English bilinguals) show different error patterns** than EFL learners — they tend to over-rely on English structures in Spanish rather than vice versa, so this guide is calibrated to EFL/ESL intermediate learners, not heritage speakers.
- **The combined "~14%" figure for article errors** used in the TL;DR is an approximation derived from adding the two distinct categories (overuse 9% + misuse 5%) reported separately in Cabrera Solano et al. (2014), and is not a directly quoted figure from the paper.
- The data sources behind this report are: Cabrera Solano et al. (2014, *English Language Teaching* 7(7)); Hevia-Tuero, Russak & Suárez-Coalla (2023, *Reading and Writing* 36(7), DOI 10.1007/s11145-022-10356-5); Hasbún (2009, *Filología y Lingüística* XXXV(2), Universidad de Costa Rica); Swan, M. & Smith, B., *Learner English: A Teacher's Guide to Interference and Other Problems* (Cambridge University Press, 2nd ed., 2001); Joseph Hudson, "10 English Pronunciation Errors by Spanish Speakers" (Pronunciation Studio, London, updated March 2018); Kester, E., *Bilinguistics* "Verb Errors of Spanish Speakers" and ASHA Leader, "Know These 4 Areas of Typical Second-Language 'Errors'"; Leonardo English; Everest Language School; ELSA Speak Blog (parts 1 and 2); Heather Hughes' ESL blog ("10 advanced-level mistakes Spanish learners make in English" and "Common English Pronunciation Problems for Spanish Speakers"); italki teacher articles; the Pronunciation Studio diagnostic; and the Berlitz, BaseLang, FluentU, SpanishDict, OnlineTeachersUK and Spanish Academy false-friend reference lists.
